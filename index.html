What Are Supplementary Angles?

Definition: Supplementary angles are two angles whose measures add up to exactly 180 degrees (180¬∞). When placed together, they form a straight angle or straight line.

Types of Supplementary Angles

1. Adjacent Supplementary Angles

Adjacent supplementary angles share a common vertex and a common side. Together, they form a straight line.

Example: When two rays extend from the same point on opposite sides of a line, the angles they form are adjacent supplementary angles.

  • Common vertex: Point C
  • Common side: Ray CB

2. Non-Adjacent Supplementary Angles

Non-adjacent supplementary angles don't share a common vertex or side, but their measures still add up to 180¬∞.

Example: An angle of 75¬∞ in one location and an angle of 105¬∞ in a different location are supplementary angles.

  • They can be anywhere
  • They don't need to connect
  • 75¬∞ + 105¬∞ = 180¬∞

Frequently Asked Questions

What are supplementary angles?

Supplementary angles are two angles whose measures add up to 180 degrees (180¬∞). They don't need to be adjacent or connected to be supplementary. The key characteristic is that their sum equals a straight angle.

How do you find the supplement of an angle?

To find the supplement of an angle, subtract the angle's measure from 180 degrees. The formula is:

Supplement = 180¬∞ - angle measure

For example, the supplement of 65¬∞ is 180¬∞ - 65¬∞ = 115¬∞

What's the difference between supplementary and complementary angles?

Supplementary angles: Add up to 180¬∞ (form a straight line)

Complementary angles: Add up to 90¬∞ (form a right angle)

The key difference is the total sum‚Äîsupplementary uses 180¬∞, complementary uses 90¬∞.

Are vertical angles supplementary?

Vertical angles are equal, not supplementary. They are opposite angles formed when two lines intersect. However, they CAN be supplementary if each vertical angle measures exactly 90¬∞.

Can two angles both be greater than 90¬∞ and still be supplementary?

Yes! For example, 100¬∞ + 80¬∞ = 180¬∞, and 110¬∞ + 70¬∞ = 180¬∞. One angle can be greater than 90¬∞ as long as the other angle is less than 90¬∞ and their sum equals 180¬∞.

Do supplementary angles have to be adjacent?

No! Supplementary angles do not have to be adjacent (next to each other). They can be anywhere‚Äîwhat matters is that their measures add up to 180¬∞. However, when supplementary angles ARE adjacent, they form a linear pair.

What are co-interior angles?

Co-interior angles (also called consecutive interior angles) are formed when a transversal intersects two parallel lines. These angles are supplementary, meaning they add up to 180¬∞. This is a key property in geometry when working with parallel lines.

How are supplementary angles used in real life?

Supplementary angles appear in architecture (roof angles), engineering (joint design), construction (angle measurements), art (perspective drawing), and many other fields. Understanding supplementary angles helps professionals calculate precise angles for various applications.
